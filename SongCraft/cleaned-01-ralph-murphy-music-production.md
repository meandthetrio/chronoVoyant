## Who's talking [00:00]

Murphy is a lifer: playing at 11, bars at 14, clubs in California and New
York, Liverpool in spring 1964, then London, where he had a record deal
within three months with Tony Hatch (writer of "Downtown," "Don't Sleep
in the Subway"). He stopped touring in 1967 (last tour with Van
Morrison, ending in the Detroit riots) after opening for the Byrds,
Small Faces, The Kinks, the Pretty Things, the Walker Brothers. Hits in
multiple genres: 60s England (Billy Fury, the Casuals, James Royal; his
own Carnaby Records label), New York (produced April Wine's first two
platinum albums; rock, metal, and a dance label), then an accidental
country hit in 1972 that took him to Nashville. He also works for ASCAP
and formed the New York Songwriters Association (1969).

His framing joke: how do you make a small fortune in the music business?
Start with a large fortune.

## The club fallacy: "I love your song" [00:03]

When you play clubs, people come up and say "I love your song" for five
reasons, in this order:

1. Your personality
2. Your vocal performance
3. The ambience of the club
4. The drink in their hand
5. Last, and least — the song

They don't mean they love the song; they mean "we love you." Buying
your CD makes it worse: it means "thank you for contributing to my good
time tonight," an extra $15 of gratitude — and when they get it home
they never play it. (Murphy on himself: he always buys the zydeco CD in
New Orleans and never plays it at home.) His producer Tony Hatch would
reject songs the club crowd had loved the night before: "It doesn't
work. It doesn't invite the listener in."

## It's all about the listener [00:04]

The hardest thing for a creative person to learn: **it's not about me.**

The joke profile of a great writer-performer: physically, sexually,
emotionally abused, from a broken home, unable to communicate, with a
desperate desire to be loved, and mildly dyslexic — "monumentally
dysfunctional." That's the good news. The bad news: **nobody cares.**

Every song you ever fell in love with is about *you*, the listener —
directly, or a situation that heavily involves you that you can relate
to. That's what a song is. Songs feel like "they read your mail."

## Whine, preach, vent [00:06]

The three things a new songwriter feels a mandate to do: whine, preach,
and vent. They are the last three things a listener wants coming out of
a speaker — **unless done with humor, irony, and detail.**

Why detail: you are creating people that never existed, in a place that
never was, doing something that never happened — and making the listener
believe it in 60 seconds. That's all the time you get.

## Songs are scripts [00:06]

You are not writing songs; you are writing **scripts for an artist to
look good to their audience.** In Murphy's lifetime, that audience has
always been women: men sing to women and women sing to women. (Men 11–23
buy hip-hop and heavy metal; if you're in the band or producing,
different story.)

Women hear differently than men speak. Two demonstrations:

- The weekend speech — "we're not going to see your mom and dad, I'll
  buy your favorite wine, cook your favorite food, your favorite songs
  and movies, bed, wine, films" — and she hears: *"you hate my
  parents."*
- The three-months-of-dating speech — long walks, same books, same
  movies, best thing in my life, forever — and she heard one word:
  *"she"* ("ever since she's been gone").

## Chekhov's gun [00:12]

Chekhov: if you put a loaded gun on stage in act one, you have to fire
it by the end of the play. When you drop details into a song, you must
explain them. The dysfunctional writer's instinct is to allude to
private things the listener has no knowledge of. Your job is to
totally explain what's in your mind, put it in coherent form, and lead
the listener appropriately through the song. That's the only kind of
song anyone will spend $1 million on — a vehicle for a singer to look
good.

Bands use the same template — beginning, middle, end, invite the
listener in — but get to choose their constituents, because they perform
to them directly.

## Always a winner [00:13]

In a song, the artist is **never a loser, always a winner.** "I'm so
lonely, I'm so miserable, such a bad life" — nobody cares.

What a song is actually for: everyone has been dissed from a great
height and thought, the next day, "I wish I'd said that." A song is the
opportunity to be the person who says it *in real time* — and the singer
looks smart and wonderful delivering it. Don't use the song as a vehicle
to make the singer look like a chump; dysfunctional writers think their
creative gift is a mandate to do exactly that. Every major act with
sustainability always *looked good* — they may have felt like weasels,
but in the songs they were winners.

"Set me up, deliver me to your hooks."

## All music is used to make money [00:14]

No music is ever played gratuitously. If someone puts speakers in a
room, they are making money by playing your work; if it didn't make them
money, they wouldn't play it.

- **Elevator music**: 1920s–30s Chicago buildings above six stories
  needed elevators; cables broke annually and killed people, so nobody
  would board. Otis invented the self-locking elevator, but the fear
  remained — until he added small speakers playing calming music. People
  stayed on board; Otis got rich.
- **Dentist office**: identifiable, comforting songs playing while they
  drill your face off; your worst-nightmare music and you'd leave.
- **Walmart**: had its own radio station, programmed to the shopper
  demographic so customers felt invited at every turn.
- **Restaurant study** (he cites an AA study run as a fake
  food-and-service evaluation with a hidden music subset): play **jazz**
  and you can charge a third more for food — jazz makes people order the
  cappuccino, the salad, the dessert, another bottle of wine.
  **Classical** feels good too, but people have four or fewer drinks and
  leave earlier — it's prim. The control group was popular music, around
  28%.

Corollary for the writer: the first time you get a check — $1, $10, $100
— for something you created, you're *validated*. It's psychologically
huge, and it matters for having a long career.

## The rights business [00:18]

- ASCAP: formed 1914 by writers, on the PRS (UK) model; owned by writers
  and publishers, 12 of each on the board, president always a writer.
  Murphy was a PRS member in England; every other country has one
  society per country, and every other country pays better.
- US radio pays about **3.23% of net/adjusted gross** to all composers
  and publishers; Canada pays 4.7% of gross plus a ~6% performance
  right — about **10.7% of gross** total. The US rate gets pushed down
  every five years.
- **BMI is owned by broadcasters** — "the people who never wanted to pay
  me have an organization to pay me." The official story (ASCAP was
  exclusionary: no Black writers, no hillbillies, no rock and roll)
  doesn't hold up: W.C. Handy — the first wholly-owned Black publishing
  company — Fats Waller, and Duke Ellington were ASCAP; BMI was born in
  1939, before rock and roll existed. The real explanation is the old
  industry expression: **"it's not the money — it's the money."**
- "Competition," per the dictionary, makes goods and services cheaper
  for consumers — why would writers want their already-cheap work made
  cheaper? (Two societies competing means writers pay for their own
  banners and lunches.)
- **New technology always kills the creator first.** Radio (1921): sheet
  music fell from a million copies to 100,000 — 90% of traditional
  income gone; people stayed home instead of going where live music
  played. Old 78s literally read "not authorized for use on radio,"
  because radio play meant no live bookings and no bar traffic. Radio
  got ASCAP's minimum 5%-of-net rate as "new technology"; by the
  mid-30s, writers asked for 7.5% and broadcasters cried bankruptcy
  (band leaders paying $4,000/year for a studio orchestra claimed $60 a
  year for arrangements would make music unaffordable).
- The "music police" complaint: a bar owner near ASCAP paid $1,800/year
  for his license — about **$6 a day** — while charging Murphy $9 for two
  beers, with live music four nights a week and ambience music setting
  the room's whole tone. General licensing is about 15% of the domestic
  check of every earning ASCAP songwriter.
- Peer-to-peer: if you bought it, do what you want with it — but people
  generally use music to make money, and if they're making money, the
  writer should too.
- Your work is like your child: nobody gets to borrow your kid to mow
  their lawn for free.

## The working writer's numbers [00:27]

Writing full-time, Murphy would finish **100 songs a year, demo about
40, average about six cuts, and get a hit every third year — a 1-in-300
margin**, working every day. On top of the creative process he spent
eight hours a day on the business: lunches with A&R, getting to know
artists, producers, labels, and **studying** — e.g., analyzing all the
previous year's #1 country records (first use of title, subject matter,
vocabulary, technology) for a Music Row article; same exercise for the
UK top 10, Australian pop vs. US pop vs. US country.

The craft is a constant across genres and generations — the craft he was
taught had been handed to his teachers 40 years earlier — only
**market forces** shape and change it. Every genre has its own language,
vocabulary, and technology; the craft underneath doesn't move.

## The artist-writer trend and the Disney model [00:28]

Of the 26 #1 country records the previous year (2008), 13 were written
in whole or part by the artist — so publishers now look for
artist-writers, then give them an underpinning of professional writers.
The Disney model: take a Miley Cyrus and staple four to six pro writers
around her as a team, so her craft is immaculate. Those records deliver
**first use of title within 60 seconds** and **four to seven repetitions
of the title** — they set you up and deliver exactly what you want.

## Craft expectation: the movie-script analogy [00:29]

You've been steeped in music since the womb — you're indoctrinated in
craft whether you know it or not, and you **expect to receive
information a certain way.** All movie scripts are written essentially
the same (introduction of characters, subplot, and so on); if a movie
doesn't deliver information the way you expect, you don't like it — no
matter how great the stars, cinematography, and sets. Songs work the
same way.

## Eight listens; melody lures, lyric keeps [00:29]

It takes about **eight listens** for a human to fully ingest a song.
First listen: you get the hook. Next: a bit of the second verse; later,
the intro. Only after hearing the whole song do you make a value
judgment — and "I love it / I hate it" is neither: either the song spoke
to you about you (or a situation that heavily involves you), or it
didn't invite you in. You don't love or hate a song; it invited you in
or it didn't.

**What brings you to a song, 100% of the time, is melody. What keeps you
there is lyric.** (You may claim not to know the lyrics, but played the
song, you'd know them.) Early rap had no melody; within two or three
years it brought melody in, because melody is what lures the listener.

A song is not "a song": it is a **linear lyrical conversation** between
the singer and every single person in the room. If you're not speaking
to them about them, forget it.

## Q&A: does theory knowledge trap you? [00:33]

A student asks whether knowing common progressions traps him into "this
must happen next." Murphy's answer: Picasso and Michelangelo both spent
seven years learning *everything exactly the same* — grinding colors,
sizing canvas, the human form, which brush when. **Once they learned
everything the same, they were free to be different.**

- A rewritten song is never a great song — always a bit lumpy — and he
  makes students rewrite as punishment, so they learn to get it right
  earlier.
- Learning craft is like learning guitar: the first D–G–A is agony; two
  years in, you're just playing.
- "It's divinely inspired, you just sit down and it comes out" is a lie
  told by failures — people who never learned the process. ("I could
  have played classical piano." "Did you ever take lessons?" "No.")

## Imitation and the Beatles [00:36]

The Beatles played covers night after night in Hamburg — they learned
where things rhymed, when the chorus arrives, vocabulary, use of
pronouns, all by imitation, across many forms. Carl Perkins (who wrote
"Matchbox") told Murphy that Paul flew him to England, sat him in a
high-backed chair for three days, and Paul, George, and Ringo sat on the
floor making him play every lick he'd ever played, over and over —
because if you really like something, figure out how they did it.

**Imitation is how you learn, and you can never actually be them — so
imitation automatically adds your own dimension.** A song is like a
pearl: many layers; every approach adds one.

## The six shapes and the rondeau [00:37]

There are **six basic shapes of the popular song**. One of the most
popular in the 30s–40s was the **rondeau** (from the symphonic form,
mutated; see the book *Sid Man* by W. Smith [unclear title], which found
the form used in Western swing to get people dancing in a heartbeat):

> chorus — verse — chorus — musical interlude — bridge — chorus — out

Only one verse in perfect rondeau form. Many dance records are
**modified rondeaus** (five of the Irish top 10 he studied were dance
records, a couple of them modified rondeaus). A dance variant: open with
two or three lines of the chorus as a splash, verses establish the
story, then chorus again.

Once you learn the structures, you modify them and move them around.
All music has a different function (his dance-label days: beats per
second, the mixes, mixer Tom Moulton spending days on one detail) — but
the common requirement is **using the appropriately structured song as
the vehicle for that record.** Know where your song fits.

## The global market [00:39]

Murphy now works as a **topline writer** — they send the track, he
writes the melody and the words — collaborating remotely (Sweden,
Norway). Don't set your sights only on New York/LA; look at the Far
East — China came to Canadian Music Week in force looking for creative
people to teach them craft. MIDEM (since 1967) lets you sit down with
publishers from Japan, Australia, Germany and find who has **passion**
for your work — because passion makes things happen, and absence of
passion is the only thing that will kill you. The more fun you have, the
more money you make (he never got in for the money). And having been
rich and poor repeatedly: rich was better.

## Vocabulary details and pronouns [00:42]

Studying #1s taught him micro-vocabulary: the added syllable that makes
a line more musical ("well" as "whale," "this" as "the-is").

At a UK college class (the ACM; the Brit School produced Amy Winehouse
and Duffy), about half of 40 students' songs made the singer look like a
loser. His fix: **change the pronouns** — "he did that / she did that"
rather than "you did that." Halfway through the class students were
handing him CDs saying "I know what's wrong."

On "we": the moment a lyric says *we*, it's preachy — "here's what we
need to do" (his pet peeve: he would never have written "We Are the
World"). The exception, as always: humor, irony, and detail ("we are
really goofy, we can have a lot of fun, so come on over tonight" — or
"how about we go for a beer").

The three questions to ask when you finish any piece of work:
**what's in it for the listener, what's in it for the listener, what's
in it for the listener.** If the answer is nothing, you're wasting your
time.
