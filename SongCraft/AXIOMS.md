## 1. The listener contract (C1, C3)

- A song is a **script**: a vehicle for a singer to look good to their
  audience — not raw self-expression. The writer gets out of the way
  ("the abdication of self").
- Every song a listener loves is about *them*, or a situation that
  heavily involves them. "I love it / I hate it" verdicts really mean
  "it invited me in" or "it didn't."
- The **club fallacy**: live praise reflects five things in order —
  personality, vocal performance, ambience, the drink in their hand,
  and last the song. Club applause is not evidence about the song.
- The **45-second rule**: 13.2–15 seconds of intro, story underway, the
  listener invited in within 45 seconds or they're gone. (C1 gives 60
  seconds; adopt the tighter C3 figure, treat 60 as the outer bound.)
- **Eight listens** to fully ingest a song; the hook lands first, the
  rest fills in across plays.
- **Three minutes is the tolerance of the human animal**; the song must
  hold the listener for its full length.
- **Melody lures, lyric keeps.** What brings a listener to a song,
  100% of the time, is melody; what keeps them is the lyric. The
  listener cannot multitask: offered both at once, they defer to melody
  100% of the time. Consequences:
  - Story passages stay melodically linear — **don't change your chord
    till you change your thought.**
  - Melody-first records take monosyllabics and open vowels, not story.
- Listeners are **indoctrinated in craft from birth** and expect to
  receive information a certain way (the movie-script analogy). Violate
  the expected delivery and they reject the song without knowing why.
- A song is a **linear lyrical conversation** with every person in the
  room — about 70% of the time a conversation between *you* and *I*.
- **Second-verse hell**: 80–90% of the time a writer's first draft
  starts too far into the story (the characters are already complete in
  the writer's head). If the song never tells the listener who these
  people are, it excludes them — and they switch it off (the party
  analogy).
- **Chekhov's gun** (both Murphy lectures): every detail planted must
  fire; every question the song raises must be answered in the song.
  The hook is the gun going off.
- **Detail is the currency of belief**: the song creates people that
  never existed in a place that never was doing what never happened,
  and detail is what makes the listener believe it in 45 seconds.
  (Connects to the truth-avoidance detector in
  specsDefined/SpecDefinitions.md — specificity is also where honesty
  lives.)
- **Title mechanics**: start from the title, stapled to the wall —
  everything in the song leads to it. Hit craft delivers first use of
  title within 60 seconds and 4–7 title repetitions. Songs are
  generally built on contrast, and can open on the opposite of the
  title.
- **Lumpy words**: never jam a three-syllable word into a two-syllable
  slot; there is always another word that fits. Singability is what
  makes a song singalong-able, and an added soft syllable
  ("well" → "whay-ell") can make a line *more* musical. (This is the
  syllable/prosody craft check, stated from the writer's side.)

## 2. Prosody: the stable/unstable toolkit (C2)

- **No rules, only tools.** Creativity is not spontaneity: it is
  selecting, among options, what fits best with everything around it
  (composition = arranging with respect to what's there). Information
  is the key to creativity.
- **Prosody is the one rule of art**: everything supports everything
  else; everything belongs and has a purpose. (This is the same word
  and concept as craft check #1 in specsDefined/SpecDefinitions.md.)
- The master diagnostic, per section and per line: **stable or
  unstable?** The build must match the feeling. **Motion creates
  emotion, always** — how a thing moves carries feeling independent of
  the words. Same denotative meaning, different setting → different
  emotion.
- The core mechanic: **raise the listener's expectations with a
  sequence, then satisfy them (stability) or thwart them
  (instability).**
- Every section has exactly **five properties**, each settable to
  stable or unstable:

| # | Property | Stable | Unstable |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Number of lines | Even | Odd (wants more; extra final repetition converts longing to completion) |
| 2 | Length of lines | Matched | Unmatched / shortened where expectation was raised |
| 3 | Rhyme scheme | AABB (couplets); ABAB (4-unit, line 3 = dominant function) | ABBA (longing, loneliness); schemes rank on a stability spectrum |
| 4 | Rhyme type | Perfect | Family → additive/subtractive → assonance → consonance (gate progressively opens) |
| 5 | Phrasing vs. downbeat | Front-heavy (starts on/into the downbeat: anchored) | Back-heavy (starts after: in motion) |

- **Perfect rhyme, three conditions**: identical vowel sounds; identical
  post-vowel consonant sounds; different syllable onsets (same onset =
  identity, not rhyme). Sound only — spelling irrelevant; accent
  doesn't break it because every speaker's vowels are self-consistent.
- **Rhymes work like chord voicings** (maps directly onto
  MusicTheory/AXIOMS.md stability): perfect rhyme = root-position tonic,
  consonance rhyme = the least-resolved inversion. Placement interacts
  with type: weakening the *dominant* position (line 3 of ABAB) softens
  the push; weakening the *resolving* position (line 4) softens the
  landing.
- **The melody-matching technique**: to make one verse stable and
  another unstable over an identical melody, keep number of lines,
  lengths, scheme, and rhythm fixed — vary only the **rhyme types**.
- **Phrasing is the body language of the song** (~7% words, ~35% tone,
  the rest body language). In 4/4 the beats weigh
  strong–weak–strong–weak; the writer is **responsible for every
  downbeat, line by line** — even skipping one mid-line changes the
  color.
- **A mediocre lyric beautifully set beats a brilliant lyric badly set,
  every day.** A line "feels great" because of its setting, not its
  content. Uniformly front-heavy phrasing = a song with no body
  language and no feeling.
- These are **rewriting tools, not premeditation** — premeditation makes
  songs sterile. The 4-line/8-bar default is a symmetry habit, not a
  law ("Yesterday" is seven bars; balancing it kills it). Things only
  need to balance when the idea is balanced.
- Once a rhyme scheme is initiated, **don't change it** (C3 agrees):
  it breaks an expectation the listener can't name but will punish.

## 3. The six shapes of the popular song (C3, C1)

Learn the forms strictly first; they are malleable *after* they're
learned — "learn everything the same and you're free to be different."

| # | Form | Structure | Listener expectation / notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Preamble song | Preamble + song | Preamble explains the song; obsolete outside musical theater — radio cut preambles off |
| 2 | Verse–chorus (expandable) | V–C–V–C–…–instrumental–C–out | **After the instrumental: chorus and out, never a third verse** — a needed third verse goes before the first chorus. Riff-based, lyrically sparse, band-written |
| 3 | Verse–chorus + middle eight | V–C–V–C–bridge–C–out | The bridge is the **"but what if?"**: it asks a question or adds information not previously in the song; after it, chorus out only. (One of the two most-used forms) |
| 4 | Verse–lift–chorus | V–lift–C–V–lift–C(–small bridge)–C | The **lift** (pre-chorus; 2/4/6 lines) lifts melodically into the chorus, holds the "and/if/but/so/maybe," creates tension, delivers the hook. Lift may repeat identically or carry new information. Maximum storytelling; recently gained a vestigial 2-line middle eight |
| 5 | AABA | verse–verse–bridge–verse, no chorus | Title is the first or last line of every verse, **consistently**. Requires time passage: "in the beginning" verse → present verse → "what if" bridge → "down the road" resolving verse. Economical; country variant adds a 4th verse with new information |
| 6 | Rondeau / rondo | C–V–C–interlude–bridge–C–out | One verse only in perfect form ("the honky tonk form"). Chorus-first fills a dance floor fast but tells the punchline first — limited shelf life; modified rondo (C–V–C) is the dance staple; the interlude expands for band soloing |

- Use the appropriately structured song as the vehicle for the record;
  know where the song fits — every form has its own listener
  expectation, and each genre's expectation must be maintained.
- Modern chorus variation: keep the chorus outline and first/last
  lines, change one interior line per chorus to keep the listener
  engaged.
- Adding new information on the way *out* of a song keeps the listener
  to the end.

## 4. Process axioms (C1, C3)

- The working numbers: ~100 songs/year → ~40 demos → ~6 cuts → a hit
  every third year. **1 in 300.** Write through the ordinary to get to
  the good; nothing you write is as good as it feels the night you
  write it. (Feeds Process-and-statistics in
  specsDefined/SpecDefinitions.md.)
- The 10,000-hours claim is adopted as true; craft is acquired first,
  then elevated (Picasso/Michelangelo: identical fundamentals, then
  free to be different).
- **Imitation is how craft is learned, and it automatically adds your
  own dimension** — you can never actually be them (Beatles/Hamburg,
  Carl Perkins). A song is a pearl: every approach adds a layer.
- Rewriting is training; a rewritten song is always a bit lumpy —
  the point is learning to get it right earlier.
- "Divine inspiration" is the excuse of those who never learned the
  process.
- Study the current #1s (first use of title, subject, vocabulary,
  structure); when one does something you'd never do, try it — then
  take it somewhere different.
- Craft is constant across genres and eras; only market forces shape
  it.
