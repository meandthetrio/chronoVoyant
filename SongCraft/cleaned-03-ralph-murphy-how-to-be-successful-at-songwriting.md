## Why Murphy teaches; ten o'clock at night versus seven in the morning [00:00]

Murphy opens by explaining why he does these talks: forty-odd years ago a group of great old writers took the time to "yell at" him and told him everything he did not want to hear. At the time he was a touring singer-songwriter playing clubs, and he believed audiences loved his songs. His mentors' correction: the reason people love your song at ten o'clock at night is a combination of five things — your personality, your singing, the ambience of the room, the drink in their hand, and the song. One "great" out of those five is enough at night. People come up and say "I love that song," but what they actually love is the combination.

That, he says, is not where the money is. He has "babies" — writers he loves such as Mary Gauthier (transcribed "Mary Goshay"), Lyle Lovett, and Joni Mitchell — who are singer-songwriters writing for the ten o'clock at night constituency. Their songs are scripts for them to be themselves to their people at ten o'clock at night, and they work perfectly for that. But seven in the morning is where the money is.

He is unapologetic about the money: his mother was a cleaning lady and his sole reason for pursuing songwriting was to buy her a house. He had stepfathers, was playing bars at eleven and fourteen [unclear — the transcript garbles the ages and his joining the musicians' union], played LA at seventeen, New York at eighteen, and was in Liverpool at twenty, buying one-way tickets everywhere because he wanted to hear other people sing his songs. He never really thought about the money, but when the first check arrived he felt validated — as a "monumentally dysfunctional human being," he was suddenly worth something.

## The songwriter's profile and the gift of perception [00:02]

Murphy gives the basic profile of songwriters in the room: physically, sexually, or emotionally abused, from a broken home, with an inability to communicate, a desperate desire to be loved, and marginally dyslexic — "pick one or all, and that's you." That dysfunction is what will make them great: writers are born with the gift of perception, the ability to look at someone and know them really well — how they stand, sit, their clothes, their shoes — and the things they have been through forge that gift into a laser beam.

Choosing the job of creator means choosing the job of giving listeners themselves as you see them. Every song you ever loved felt like it was all about you — as if the writer read your mail and tapped your phone. That requires what Murphy calls the abdication of self: you have to get yourself out of the way.

## Whine, preach, vent — and the discipline of detail [00:03]

The three things a writer with this gift feels mandated to do are whine, preach, and vent. Those are the last three things Murphy wants coming out of his speaker — unless they are done with humor, irony, and detail, detail, detail.

Why detail? Because a songwriter is creating people that never existed, in a place that never was, doing something that never happened — and making the listener believe it in 45 seconds. You do not have three minutes to win the listener; you have 45 seconds.

Writing is part of a cathartic process — you write because otherwise you would need a shrink, and maybe you can make a buck out of your dysfunction — but you have to get past the whining, preaching, and venting. Every song you fell in love with invited you in and made you feel part of its fabric. You have about 60 seconds to do that.

Examples of detail (from the Q&A and country examples he cites):

- "Live Like You Were Dying": it is not the singer who has cancer, it is a guy he knows; the song is loaded with detail and minutiae — the bull named Fu Man Chu, 2.7/3.7 seconds [transcript says 3.7] — and everyone connects with it.
- "Before He Cheats" (written by two of his "babies," Josh and Chris, who won a Grammy for it): the singer is venting, but with humor and dense detail (the Shania karaoke, the men's-room polo references from the lyric), so listeners go along with it.
- From the Q&A: when two women have lunch and one says "he left me," the other asks whether he is working out, whether he has a new car, a haircut, new clothes — women want all the accompanying detail. When two men have lunch and one says "she left me," the other says "that's a drag, have a beer" — end of conversation. Detail, humor, and irony are huge, and women are particularly sensitive to detail; they want to be told the stuff.

## Who the listener is: demographics [00:05]

Motown is Murphy's model of listener-focused writing. He knew the operation from the inside — he stayed at the "gaudy" mansion (formerly the Japanese embassy) with its ten-pin AMF bowling alley and marble swimming pool, and his company did the print work for Motown with Berry Gordy (transcribed "Bobby Gordy" (sp?)). Everything on a Motown record was geared for the listener, and that listener was always a woman, 100% of the time. Via Valerie Simpson of Ashford and Simpson (who sits on the ASCAP board), he learned Motown had a committee that sat around a table examining how quickly each song got to the first use of the title and how it invited the person in. He cites "Papa Was a Rolling Stone" and "Love Child" as detail-heavy examples.

The demographic claims:

- Women physically buy 50% of all records made and make men buy the other 50%.
- The only areas where males dominate as buyers are ages 11–23 — rap, hip-hop, thrash metal, grunge — where the male is in the band; and males 50–65, who buy jazz and symphonic.
- In the band-driven genres you must be part of the band (artist, producer, everyone writes within the project) — a standalone writer cannot pitch songs there because they will not record outside songs. If you are part of such a project, do it well.
- Therefore Murphy, as a standalone writer, writes for men to sing to women and for women to sing to women.

The industry will spend one million dollars on your song, and the song is a script to make the artist look good to women at the worst possible time of day.

## Drive time: the woman at seven in the morning [00:07]

The template is the same in England, Australia, Canada, New York, and LA, because drive time is universal worldwide: 6:30 to 8:30. Murphy picks the morning because he hates mornings.

At seven o'clock in the morning a woman wants exactly three things: local news, local weather, and local traffic. In that one-hour window — while she is getting to a job she hates, getting kids to school, getting her old man out the door — there are 18 minutes of advertising (Coca-Cola, Ford trucks), morning-show chatter, and in the middle of that quagmire radio inserts a brand-new song nobody knows. Listeners have a predisposition to hate it; they are waiting for a reason to hate it.

So you must invite them in within 45 seconds: 13.2 to 15 seconds of intro, 45 seconds of story. If you have not invited the listener in within 45 seconds, they are gone.

From the Q&A, his ideal-listener template: when you go fishing you think like the fish, not the fisherman, so when Murphy listens to songs he does not listen as an older male — he thinks like a woman, 25 to 40, driving to a job she hates, in a relationship with a man she does not care for, having just dropped her kids at daycare, in the fast lane on the interstate, in the rain, in a car about to break down — and the song has to hold her attention for three minutes and thirty seconds. That is heavy lifting, and it is deliberate: if you can win her, you have everybody. Like a marathoner training on the hardest part of the course, pick the hardest goal and the rest looks easy.

## The enemy across the table: the job-interview parallel [00:10]

Teaching in Vancouver, Murphy found a Globe and Mail article that crystallized the situation, titled (as he presents it) "The enemy across the table." The listener is the enemy: you have to win them over when they do not want to be won over.

The parallel: your song getting on radio is like being called in for a job interview. Getting the interview does not mean you have the job — the interviewer will lay traps to trick you into revealing one good reason to eliminate you (he cites career coach Cynthia Shapiro, author of a book on getting hired). Radio listeners are likewise waiting for you to look like an idiot. Example trap: to women, when a relationship ends it is 100% of the time the man's fault — so if a male star sings "since you left me," every woman thinks "asshole," and the singer is gone on the first line.

Rules Murphy draws from the interview article:

- Never give an answer or share an experience that does not have a happy ending, does not end successfully, or does not showcase you as a star. The standalone writer's job is to make every artist the star. A song is your opportunity to be the person who, in real time, says the thing you wished you had said the day after someone humiliated you.
- The halo effect: we tend to trust people we perceive are like us. If the singer is likable to women, or like that woman, listeners feel good; mirroring body language, tone of voice, and pace of speaking creates a halo.
- Never admit you have been fired: the moment you disclose it, the interview is effectively over because it raises questions about your reliability. Even if a previous experience ended badly, just say you gained valuable experience.
- Interviewers remember stories more than lists of achievements — so tell me a story, with a beginning, a middle, and an end.
- Prepare three or four stories about career achievements, 100% positive, showcasing you as a star, maximum three minutes. Murphy points out that describes a record: three minutes is the tolerance of the human animal.

He notes that none of this was "just from the heart" at Motown, and the same machinery existed on Denmark Street behind the Beatles (he knows George Martin and knew "the boys") and behind Disney's Hannah Montana operation. It is a machine.

## The singer is always a winner: point of view and pronouns [00:11]

The rule of thumb: the singer must always be a winner. The singer is never a loser and never over 30. Your first instinct — "I'm so miserable" — is wrong; make it "he's so miserable." Change to third person: if there is a loser in a song, it is somebody else.

In "Brown Eyed Girl" terms (from the AABA discussion): you sing "when we're old and gray" — you are never actually old in the song, because you are never a loser and never over 30 in a song, ever. If you hear a song where the singer is a loser over 30, know that the artist wrote it and the A&R people were not strong enough to say no. It is a bad career move — a "death trap" — and you do not survive many of them, because people do not want to see you not be a winner. You are on stage, in the entertainment business: entertain. Make the listener feel good about themselves — "I feel bad enough about me already."

From the Q&A, the general rule for he/she versus I/you: whenever the person in the song is over 30, a loser, or the material is preachy, use he and she — describe the loser couple, then draw a conclusion from it, which makes the singer look good to the audience ("I like to beat my wife" is unacceptable; "he likes to beat his wife," followed by the singer's conclusion, makes the singer the cool truth-teller). I/you, when appropriate, has more impact because "you" invites the listener in.

Playing the "you card": once you sing "you" — "if you were here it would all be different" — you had better tell the listener how great they are (you would make the birds sing, you would make the rain come). Once you open that door, spell it out; the listener wants to know exactly how they changed your life. (He points to the fourth-form example he plays, where the singer says "you make my world go on" and lists the wonderful things she is, all within about 60 seconds.)

An artist with a label deal recently asked Murphy to advise him. Murphy asked: who is your audience? Who are you singing to? Given the demographic, the audience expects certain things — and the artist's song was whiny and made him look bad. Murphy's fix: make it third person; instead of "you left me," write "she left him," then draw a conclusion from it. Write smart — it is a job, you are the underpinning of the music business, and a million dollars will be spent on what you create.

Also under the winner rule: whiny victim songs are a trap. "Ever since you beat me up" — women as victims — gets deleted. If a woman superstar sings that her man beats her, the woman listener at seven in the morning thinks: this is supposed to be my hero and she is getting her ass kicked by this jerk — what kind of fool are you? It diminishes the singer. It may be true, but the listener does not want to hear it. ("Believing makes it so. Never let the truth get in the way of a good story. Ever.")

Writers who only whine at ten o'clock at night can be pushed out of the food chain — they will never be competitors, locked into playing 200 dates a year for the rest of their lives. Murphy's dear friends John Prine (with whom he was about to go fishing; his partner Roger Cook had two country number ones with Prine, "Love Is On a Roll" and "I Just Wanna Dance With You"), Lyle Lovett, and Mary Gauthier write for ten o'clock at night and love it. But some of those artists, around age 50, come to him saying they cannot do 200 more dates a year and need hits — and none of their songs work at seven in the morning. It is a different craft: retooling everything to accommodate the listener at the worst time of day, which requires thinking like a woman at seven in the morning. Like fishing: if you are told the fish bite in eight feet of water on red worms in the morning, and you go out in 30 feet of water at three in the afternoon with blue worms, you are stupid.

## Songwriting as a profession and a craft [00:14]

Murphy tells the class he does not care if they do not want hits — "you don't need me not to be successful." His late friend Harlan Howard (180 hits; wrote "I Fall to Pieces," "Busted," "Tiger by the Tail") teased him for teaching seminars — when Murphy was president of the Nashville Songwriters Association he ran outreach seminars in Colorado, Montana, and elsewhere — accusing him of teaching "mouth-breathing knuckle-draggers" to take their jobs. Murphy's answer: he is showing people how hard it is, so they respect that songwriters are craftspeople.

The industry is an inverted pyramid balanced on the song. Take the song away and everything goes away: piano manufacturers, tour buses, theaters, ticket sellers, lighting people. That is also why songwriters are so resented and why people trivialize the work ("anybody can write a song") — to which Murphy replies: anyone can do brain surgery, want to come over tonight and we'll cut your head open? He tells the story of a doctor on a plane who said "I'm a songwriter too" though she had written nothing anyone knew; Murphy replied that by her logic putting band-aids on his kid's finger makes him a doctor. Someone who writes songs is not a songwriter: a songwriter is a professional who knows their niche market, how to explore it, and what their music will do.

Knowing where your music fits is part of the education. Country music does not fit film and television, because a country song is a little mini-movie in 2:30 (like "The Gambler"); play one behind a movie scene and the viewer's attention diverts to the song and starts running the song's own movie. A lot of music does not function in particular areas.

Music is always used to make money — the only time anyone plays music is to make money:

- The Otis elevator story (from his morning class): high-rise buildings in Chicago needed elevators, but people would not board because elevators crashed. Otis invented the self-locking elevator; still no one would board. Then he put in a telephone and speakers playing very calming music — everyone stayed on board, and elevator music made Otis rich.
- Walmart has its own radio station and figured out the demographic of its shoppers, playing music appropriate to that age group.
- Dentists' offices play comfy, warm, fuzzy music; thrash metal would send patients running because it is inappropriate for the setting.
- Classical music in a restaurant: you pay a third more for food but have fewer drinks and leave earlier. Jazz: you pay a third more for food and you stay — coffee, cappuccino, dessert, salad — because it makes you feel expansive and invites you in.

Your job as a creator is to know the appropriate niche for you.

## Working numbers, odds, and the 10,000 hours [00:20]

Not everything you create will be great. When Murphy wrote full time he finished 100 songs a year, demoed about 40, averaged about six cuts, and got a hit every third year — a 1-in-300 margin. The old adage about 10,000 hours is absolutely true: to be great at anything, invest the 10,000 hours. But you also have to write smart.

## Second-verse hell: why you write the second verse first [00:21]

Murphy says that probably 90% of the time (he later says 80%) you will write the second verse first, and there is a reason rooted in who you are: by the time you get the killer idea and find your laptop, pen, or crayons, you have already created the characters in your head — where they went to school, what they had for lunch, how many kids they have. So your pen hits the page at a point in the action already down the road: she comes up from the kitchen table, slaps his face, gets to the yellow car, love is over.

Then comes what he calls second-verse hell: "I've said it all, nowhere to go." The problem is the listener was never told who these people are. Who is he? The captain of the football team. Where does the song say that? It doesn't. Who is she? The cheerleader. Where does it say that? It doesn't. Where did the yellow car come from? It doesn't say.

The party analogy: if you are at a party where everybody knows everybody and you know no one, after ten minutes standing by the door you leave — you were excluded. When you do not invite the listener into your song — do not tell them who the people are and what is going on — you exclude them, and they exclude you, because it is easy to flip a switch and turn you off.

## Pronouns: the linear lyrical conversation [00:23]

A song, 70% of the time, is a linear lyrical conversation between two people: you and I. He, she, they, and them are devices that come between you, us, I, and we — they are used in conflict.

Writers habitually pick the wrong pronoun (men writing "I love her, she's the greatest"; women writing "I love him, he's so cool"). Teaching in England years ago, Murphy said he did not know why this happens, and a hand went up at the back of the class — a Harley Street psychiatrist — who explained: because we are monumentally dysfunctional, we are not good at relationships. Singing about "him" is easier than singing to "you"; "you" is too much to handle. "You and I, we're in love" is harder for a dysfunctional person than "he and I are in love."

At a broadcasters' seminar in Calgary two years ago, three new acts each played two songs, all with the wrong pronouns. Murphy asked a broadcaster (now president of the Canadian Country Music Association) what she thought of a "she's so wonderful" song: "it's all right." He asked how she would have felt had it been "you — I love you": "that'd be cool." On the next act, he had the whole table mentally substitute "you" and "us" all the way through, and they said: wow, that's a different song.

The biggest problem you will ever have as a creator is you: your goofy, whiny, preachy, dysfunctional self butting in to tell the world how miserable you are. The last thing anyone wants to hear at seven in the morning is how miserable you are (at ten o'clock at night, with a glass of wine, it is tolerable).

## Learning the rules to be free [00:28]

Murphy studies all of this for himself and shares it because he wants students to know everything he knows — and then he wants to "kick your ass" and get the cut anyway. He quotes the old English expression "in the valley of the blind, the one-eyed man is king" but says he wants everyone fully sighted; he still wants the number one record, but he wants you to have that shot too, because his mother was a cleaning lady and he wants your dreams to have a chance. Once you get over your own dysfunctional whining and demystify the process, it is fun. Once you know the rules, you can play.

The football analogy: imagine being dressed in a uniform and sent onto the field with no idea how the game is played — you would get crushed and humiliated. Many people come to songwriting thinking it is easy, get humiliated, and get angry at the process, because everyone lied to them at ten o'clock at night ("I love your song" meaning personality, performance, ambience, drink). This is one reason people leave small towns for big cities — Murphy ended up in New York, London, then Nashville, retooling and relearning — and one reason he teaches in London, Toronto, Vancouver, and LA: locals are put down at home by people who say "anyone can do that." They cannot. It is a craft, acquired and then elevated.

His standing illustration: Picasso and Michelangelo were exactly the same — both had to learn to size a canvas, grind their own colors, handle a palette knife, when to use a horsehair brush versus a camelhair brush; the early work of both was pots of flowers. Once they had learned everything the same, they were free to be different. Similarly, all great guitar players share a genesis in common heroes and then translate that into their own way: his friend Randy Bachman (Bachman-Turner Overdrive's "Takin' Care of Business"; with the Guess Who, "American Woman," "These Eyes") credits many great old guitar players whose stuff he took and evolved "in his own weird lumpy way." You imitate and then you grow from that.

## Three minutes: the tolerance of the human animal [00:32]

Three minutes is the tolerance of the human animal. Harlan Howard used to say only a dumbass takes more than three minutes to tell anything. When Harlan died, Murphy was asked to do the eulogy. He hates eulogies because he is a crier — he once broke down three lines into a eulogy for a young keyboard-player friend — and he tried to get Willie Nelson to do it instead (Willie knew Harlan better). Remembering Harlan's three-minute rule, he compressed the seven-to-eight-minute eulogy down to three minutes, timed it with an egg timer standing in his shorts by the stove, and eulogized a man with 180 hits in exactly three minutes on the Ryman stage — because that is the expectation of the listener.

## The six basic shapes of the popular song [00:33]

There are six basic shapes of the popular song. Each has its own area, and each could fill a whole day of teaching, but Murphy walks through the six forms and the listener expectation attached to each. He stresses at the end of the sequence: all these structures are malleable — once you learn them and become invested in them, you can start asking "what if I...?" and move things around; that is when you inject your personality, vision, and perspective. When he works with beginners he enforces the forms religiously, like teaching guitar, until the student starts asking "what if I did that?"

### First form: the preamble song

Almost never used anymore; used a lot in musical theater. There is a preamble that tells you basically all about the song before the song proper starts. In "I Left My Heart in San Francisco," the preamble is about all the places (Paris, Rome, Manhattan) that did not turn the singer on; the rest is all about San Francisco. Friends of Murphy's who wrote in the era when the publisher was king (writing nine to five at the piano, turning out five or six songs a week) would write the part we know today as the song, play it for the publisher, and the publisher would say "great, go finish it" — meaning write the explanation. "Stardust" is another example with a preamble. Radio hated preambles — legato, out of tempo, they did not fit — so radio cut them off and all you heard was the rest of the song. "Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer" originally had the front section naming the other reindeer to explain how Rudolph got there, because there was no Rudolph in the tradition — they made him up ("believing makes it so"). Now the preamble is unnecessary because everyone accepts that of course there is a Rudolph.

### Second form: verse–chorus (expandable)

Verse, chorus, verse, chorus, verse, chorus, an instrumental, chorus, and out — with as many verse-chorus pairs as you want. Used a lot in dance, hip-hop, folk, and in country to tell stories; it is an expandable form. In rock and roll it is generally verse, chorus, verse, chorus, instrumental, chorus, out. The listener expectation: after the instrumental there is almost never a third verse — once you hear the instrumental you expect chorus and done. If you need a third verse, tuck it in before the first chorus. (Every once in a while a song breaks this — aberrant and weird, like the occasional 15-minute hit — but per the old English expression, the race is not always won by the swiftest, "but that's the way to bet.")

It is a dominant musical form, often based on a riff, without a lot said lyrically, can be obscure, and is generally written by the band — very hard to participate in unless you are a member of the band. Once you hit the instrumental section, the song is, for the listener, effectively over. Played example: Linkin Park, "Shadow of the Day" — verse, chorus, verse, chorus, instrumental, chorus, out.

### Third form: verse–chorus with a middle eight (bridge)

Verse, chorus, verse, chorus, then a middle eight or bridge (England says "middle eight," not "bridge"). Third and fourth forms are the most used forms. The middle eight/bridge is known as the opportunity to say "but what if?" — it adds another dimension, and it does one of two things: asks the question, or adds information not previously included in the rest of the song.

Played example: a country song [title not stated in the transcript] co-written by Ashley Gorley (Murphy could not recall the co-writers' names; he works with Ashley a lot). Points he draws from it: the amount of detail (the SUV, sitting in front of the school, putting the car in park, the ages of the kids, specific numbers like "436, 123" [unclear — plausibly house numbers/figures from the lyric]); and the point-of-view craft — most writers would have made the singer preach the message ("you're going to miss this") directly to the little brother, with mom saying it in verse one, dad in verse two, the plumber in the middle eight; instead the singer just reports what they were saying, so the singer is not preachy. After the bridge, all the listener expects is chorus out — no more information. He also notes the chorus put "a chord on every word," and mentions his friend Paul [unclear name — transcribed "Paul Wims" (sp?)], writer of monster hits, who writes in monosyllabics because one-syllable words invite the listener in and the ease of singing is a huge item.

Second played example: the Ting Tings [song title not stated; described as the girl who has been sitting on the fence, all dressed up with nobody paying attention, who finally loses it]. Murphy uses it to show adding new information on the way out of the song: that happens a lot, because you have to keep the listener for the full three minutes and thirty seconds "all the way to the Ford commercial" — and hold them for the chart life of the record. Asked about the 28 weeks figure, he says the life of a pop song to number one is actually 22 weeks on average, 28 weeks [unclear — the exchange garbles which figure denotes what]. He studied all the country number ones of the previous year for an article: they went up and down really fast, and there were 26 number ones where normally there are 22 to 24.

### Fourth form: verse–lift–chorus

Verse, then a section called a lift, channel, climb, or pre-chorus (in England, confusingly, called a bridge): two, four, or six lines preceding the chorus or title line. Then chorus; verse, lift, chorus. Murphy calls it a lift because it lifts you melodically into the chorus; it is also where you position your "and, if, but, so, maybe" — it has multiple functions, creating tension as it leads you in and then delivering you to the hook. Huge songs use it: "Burning Love," Leona Lewis (transcribed "Leon Lewis"), Sara Bareilles' "(I'm Not Gonna Write You a) Love Song."

In the last decade writers have started adding a middle eight to fourth form — a "but what if"/new-information section, but very small, vestigial, two lines — then lift-chorus or just chorus and out, with a little instrumental if you choose. It is one of the most complex forms and gives the maximum storytelling. Murphy noticed that in England four of the top 10 were written in fourth form.

A structural variable inside the form: the lift can repeat the same lyric each time, or carry new information — that flexibility is part of the form's freedom. In "Bleeding Love" the lift changes; in Murphy's own hit for Crystal Gayle, "Don't Take Me Half the Way" [transcript garbles the title as "Half the Way" phrasing], he kept the lift the same because he liked it the way it was.

Played examples:

- An apology song [unclear which song; the singer repeatedly apologizes — "I'm sorry I'm bad... I'm sorry I'm Bill" [unclear]] — verse, lift, chorus, verse, lift, chorus, bridge, chorus, out. It has humor, detail, and irony; it makes the character look cool; and it demonstrates the you-card rule — when the singer says "you make my world go on," he immediately tells her all the wonderful things she is, within about 60 seconds.
- Sara Bareilles, "Love Song": the story behind it is that she delivered her album and the label said it heard no hits and asked for a mid-to-uptempo love song; she refused to "write butter," sat down, and wrote "Love Song" — a monster hit, which bought her freedom (though Murphy notes no one can ever really do what they want; it is about what the audience wants and knowing the expectation). The record is full of information; the opening line (the head-under-water image) is classic smart writing; the pronoun "you" invites the listener in; the lift talks to every woman; she is not whining ("I know all about you"); and she adds new information at the very end (the "seven seas" line). Women hear it as a script: I'm not going to give it up until you do this and this — she looks very cool to women.
- Leona Lewis, "Bleeding Love": Murphy was teaching in England about two years earlier when it was released, broke it down for the class, and calls it immaculate fourth form, brilliantly written. He brought it back to the States and phoned producers urging them to cut it on an American act. When he first heard it he knew it was a monster because it was perfectly written — the "frozen" image right at the beginning, the early use of "you." That is the kind of song that gets used in jingles and movies and lasts a long, long time.

All of these songs are singing to women and always making the character look cool. Murphy adds: Shania Twain cut one of his songs long ago, so he got invited to her concerts — there were guys there, but they had tickets because their partner loved her, or they brought their daughters. You can write all the metal you want, but unless you are in the band you will not get it cut, and you will be very frustrated; this whole talk is about not being frustrated.

### Fifth form: AABA

An endearing, warm, fuzzy form that is deceptively easy-looking and very hard to write in: the old A-A-B-A. There is no chorus, and the title is the first or last line of the verse — whichever you choose, you must be consistent throughout. (Asked whether "no chorus" really means a refrain, Murphy agrees: it is the refrain/title sung as the conclusion to each verse, as in "Brown Eyed Girl," the biggest illustration everyone knows.)

Consistency here is like rhyme scheme: once you initiate a rhyme scheme, you do not change it, because you have created an expectation in the listener's ear. If you change it you confuse them — like a movie that withholds information the audience expects to receive in a certain way; the listener will not even know why they do not like the song, they just will not feel comfortable.

A sense of time passage is essential for AABA; with a static sequential situation "you're screwed." The architecture:

- First verse: the "in the beginning" verse (e.g., schoolyard, pulled your ponytail, threw a frog at you — "that's why I love you").
- Second verse: the present ("now I'm here with you every day...").
- Bridge (middle eight): "but what if" (e.g., what if I'd never met you).
- Third and resolving verse: the "on down the road, when I'm old and gray" verse — in "Brown Eyed Girl," casting the memory back. And note: you say "when we're old and gray" because you are never old in a song.

Played example: Miley Cyrus, "See You Again" — fifth form, no chorus, with a little middle-eight/bridge section and then a third and resolving verse. Using the Disney model, great writers who know structure wrote it so tight "it squeaks," making it immediately accessible. They collected Miley's actual catch phrases ("my sister says...", the way she talks) so the vocabulary is taken exactly from her as a human being, consistent with the image her fan base of young girls has of her — not an adult vocabulary; the perfect script for her to be "Miley Cyrus" to the 12-year-olds. And she is a winner in it: she freaks out, cannot look at the boy, but she is going to redeem herself ("can't wait to see you again"), and every girl cheers her on. From a certain point the listener expects nothing new — it is a very economical form.

Variants: you can go back and repeat one of the verses; and in a lot of country now they add a fourth verse — verse, verse, middle-eight section, third verse, back to middle eight (possibly with new information), then a fourth verse with new information.

Murphy's friend Richard Leigh (transcribed "Richard Lee") claims he is "not structured" yet writes in this form all the time — "Don't It Make My Brown Eyes Blue," "Somewhere in My Broken Heart," "The Greatest Man I Never Knew" — hit after hit, all AABA; the few times he and Murphy have written together over 20–30 years, he sits down and writes fifth form.

### Sixth form: rondo

Rondo comes from the symphonic tradition and appeared in Western swing; Harlan Howard called it "the honky tonk form." Structure: start with a chorus, go to a verse, back to the chorus, a musical interlude, then the middle eight (bridge), chorus and out. There is only one verse in perfect rondo form, and perfect rondos almost never become big hits. In Europe they do modified rondo — chorus, verse, chorus (Kylie Minogue (sp? — transcribed "Kali Minogue") and similar acts) — and because they start with the chorus they get people onto the dance floor very quickly. The drawback: it is like telling a joke by giving the punchline first, so the shelf life is very limited — but they make great dance records. A recent country example: "Chicken Fried" by the Zac Brown Band, which starts with the chorus; Billboard marveled that it was one of the biggest dance records of the year, and Murphy's reaction is "duh" — it was designed for the human animal to get up and dance, and it fulfilled that function. There are no mysteries.

Murphy's own rondo story: Paul Brady (who wrote "The Long Goodbye" for Brooks & Dunn, wrote with Ronan Keating for the boy band Boyzone, and writes a lot with [unclear — transcribed "Bonnie Wright" (sp?)]; Murphy got him into co-writing years ago) insisted they finally write together. Murphy resisted — his machine was rusty from teaching (citing the old adage [unclear — transcribed "read the teacher you do," presumably the those-who-can't-teach adage]; teaching locks you into thinking mechanically, and going back out to work frees you from the dogma). They agreed to write for fun in different structures, and Brady did not know what rondo was. Murphy's idea: a guy going to church sees a new girl in town singing in the choir and cannot wait one more day; needing detail, Murphy calculated the number of minutes, seconds, and hours in a day and used that for the chorus. The demo, "One More Day," sung by Brady, starts with the chorus (the seconds-in-a-day figures), goes to the verse, chorus, a little instrumental, then — instead of returning to a chorus — straight into the bridge ("was that an angel in the choir?"), then chorus, chorus, out. In the thirties and forties in Western swing, bands would expand that little instrumental section and blow on it for ten minutes — 24 bars of sax, 24 of guitar, bass, a little drum thing — then come back in at the bridge. Great for bands, but such songs are harder to get cut and harder to have big hits with, because the structure gives away the chorus right off the top.

## Melody versus lyric [00:44]

What will bring you to a song, 100% of the time, as a listener is the melody. What will keep you there is the lyric — the story. The human animal cannot multitask: you can hear one thing or the other, not absorb both completely, and given the preference the human animal will always defer to melody, 100% of the time.

Consequences:

- Where the record is all melody — writing for Mariah Carey or Celine Dion — you use little monosyllabics, open vowel sounds, consonants for the title. No one cares about the story; the divas want singable stuff. Fine — but do not try to strap a story in there.
- Wherever you are telling a story, you have no melody, because melody would steal the listener; you remain linear. Don't change your chord till you change your thought. This is why rap cannot coexist with melody: the moment you go into melody, the human animal defers to the melody and loses the thread of the story.
- You must maintain the integrity of the listener's expectation for each genre.

Illustration: "I Believe in You" (Don Williams), written by Murphy's partner Roger Cook with [unclear — transcribed "Sammy Hilgen" (sp?)], which Murphy published. The verses are a laundry list of things the singer does and doesn't believe in, delivered in a linear pattern so you concentrate on the lyric; when it reaches the title sentiment — which is "basically saying nothing" — the melody comes in. Murphy also sings a line from another record ("working as a waitress in a small cafe...") to show a story staying linear until the story point lands. Where you tell your story, remain linear; but you have to have melody to lure the listener in — be judicious, set it up.

## Lumpy words and singability [00:47]

Singer-songwriters especially must beware what Murphy calls lumpy words — speed bumps: words that do not fit the slot. Resist the urge. Accessibility is huge; inviting the listener in is huge. Every song you fell in love with growing up was easy to sing — you could sing right along — which means the writer did their job and took out the speed bumps. There is always another way to say exactly the same thing with a word that fits: if the slot is two syllables, do not jam a three-syllable word in. You can jam it in — don't. It makes you look like a dumbass to professional writers, who will cringe while being polite ("that's very interesting").

(This echoes his London apprenticeship: Tommy Connor would rip apart Murphy's overnight lyrics at breakfast — "there's three syllables in a two-syllable spot, get that rubbish out of there" — and would not accept "but it says it better.")

## Starting a song: the title on the wall [01:43]

From the Q&A: Murphy generally starts with a title. Take the title, put it on a piece of paper, and staple it to the wall: everything from here leads to that title — everything. Look at the title and ask how you get there; what is the story? Find an original story; do not talk vaguely — set up a story. Or do the opposite of the title: if the title is "It's Been Raining and Cold Since You've Been Gone," open with blue skies and birdsong when the lover was there. Songs are generally built on contrast ("I love you but he's back in town"; "I love you and we're going to the moon") and can pivot on it.

Chekhov said it best: the loaded gun in act one, scene one — then you fire it. The hook is the gun going off, so you have to put the gun in there. Never include anything that is not explained fully in the song, and somewhere in the song you must answer every question you raise. No unexplained stuff — you are engaging women at the worst time of day possible, and they want to know.

## Business material and the writing life (Q&A) [01:25]

**Are lyrics de-emphasized today?** No. People say there were so many great hits in the 60s and 70s — there weren't; there has always been a lot of rubbish. The ones that endure are the ones that have all the elements. If you write a big hit with all the ingredients, intelligently written, it will be your pension plan — there is no pension plan for songwriters; your copyright is your pension plan. Written well, a song will walk the earth for 70 years after you are dead, paying for your great-grandchildren's surgery, college, and cars — if you take care of it.

**Karaoke and calling cards.** Murphy wants to write songs everyone sings — in hallways, from car radios, at karaoke. He despises karaoke, but hearing a stranger sing his song on a karaoke machine means he did his job: he invited listeners in and got his "whiny, preachy, venting, dysfunctional butt" out of the way. Writing songs for yourself is easy — you can knock out ten a night; that does not make you a genius, just whiny and dysfunctional. Once in a while such a song can even be a hit; he calls those "calling cards." Joni Mitchell's gigs are advertised on radio with "Big Yellow Taxi" and "Both Sides Now," not the thousandth obscure cut; if an act with one big hit does not play it, the audience is unhappy. Murphy plays in the round for charities, and if the writers do not play their hits he demands them (he will not let Richard leave without "Don't It Make My Brown Eyes Blue" and the dog story [unclear]). You want to hear the song from the creator's mouth. He mentions playing the following Friday with "Cowboy" Jack Clement, writer of "Ballad of a Teenage Queen," who was turning 78 the next week.

**Who do I pitch songs to?** You can take songs to publishers, but in the main: write and write and write and write. Tia Sillers (transcribed "Tia Silers") told of signing with a publisher who gave her money and said: I don't want to see you for a year; go away and write every day; come back with what you've got; all I'm looking for is one killer — one great song in a year makes all my money back. Everything you write is not wonderful; it is called writing through the ordinary to get to the good. Everything you write feels like the greatest thing on the planet — it is not. If at the end of his life Murphy has 30 songs he thinks are just great, it will be the product of 45 years. A year and a half earlier he had the number two record in England with Cliff Richard, almost exactly 40 years after his first number one; he has had hits in five decades — not because he is smart, but because he was taught craft and cares enough to write past the ordinary to the good stuff.

He never made it out of high school and used to carry a dictionary everywhere; when he did not know something, he would say "let's find out together." Because he is "so stupid" he learns new things every day, so he will be forever 18.

**You can't stop a great song.** If you write a really great song and play out, Murphy will probably know about it within three months. In the old days in New York, if someone great was playing in the Village, everyone heard within a week. When Jimi Hendrix came to London, within a week everybody in London knew — and London's hard-asses were not forgiving. Murphy had breakfast with him one morning at the Gioconda (transcribed "Gia Konda"), the breakfast place where everybody sat at shared tables. Murphy was doing a paper round at four in the morning because his first writing gig paid three pounds nineteen and six (about $8 a week) while his rent was $9 a week; the round bought him ten cigarettes, lunch, and bus fare. Most mornings he showed his previous night's lyrics to Tommy Connor — the great old writer of "I Saw Mommy Kissing Santa Claus," which was banned for three years in the United States because radio thought it depicted Mommy having an affair (it's Santa's the dad, "hello") — and Tommy tore them apart. Hendrix sought Tommy out and joined them; Murphy did not know who he was — "some guy with big hair." Within a week everyone knew; he played the little pub off Oxford Street and every guitar player in town turned up — Clapton, the Stones — and within a month he was a hero, because he was that good, that original. You can't stop greatness. But great songs are few and far between: you have to write a lot of muck before you find the diamonds ("nuggets," as he calls them).

**Murphy's credits (asked about his hits).** His first was "Call My Name" by James Royal. He produced April Wine's first two platinum albums around 1970 (the band was just inducted into the Hall of Fame in Canada). He wrote the number one "Don't Take Me Half the Way" [unclear phrasing] for Crystal Gayle and "He Got You" for [unclear — artist name garbled as "Aunty Mel Staff"]. He did not start in country until 1971 — he accidentally had a country hit while doing movie work; he mentions writing for a film [unclear — transcript garbles this passage: "Love River Phoenix... she plays in the bluebird, the woman's song, God's a Woman"]. He loves doing what he does but has a low threshold of boredom — once he has done something he wants to move on.

**The ASCAP job.** When ASCAP approached him he had just turned 50 and said he did not want a job; asked what they wanted him to do, they said: help, teach, and work with people. He said, you're going to pay me to do what I do for free? It gave him a wonderful opportunity to put back into the community. His job: helping writers who come in, advising publishers on who to look at, and supporting his big writers between deals. Often he will "bust a writer's chops" for three to five years, get them a deal, they have a couple of hits — and immediately say, now I can do what I want. You can never do what you want as a writer. You can tinge — bring your personal influences to what the industry wants — but in the long run you always give them what they want. The consumer is like a restaurant customer: if he doesn't want fish, he won't order fish; if he wants steak, he'll go to another restaurant. Listeners do not buy what they are told to buy: you can play a record on radio for three months and if people do not want it — if it does not talk to them about them or invite them in — they will not buy it.

**Bands, fashion, and radio politics.** With bands, a lot of it is fashion, driven by touring. Radio is driven by demand: when listeners complain that a big band (e.g., U2) is never played anymore, radio finds the least offensive song on the current album and plays it just to placate the listenership. The song does not really work; it gets to about number 20 and dies — lip service paid because the band has a huge following. Such bands may do something really cool at ten o'clock at night live, but their songs do not work at seven in the morning. The big songs, though, cannot be stopped.

**Is the Nashville songwriting business changing (Clear Channel, spins)?** It isn't. Murphy has noticed structures changing, but only marginally: fourth form never used to have a middle eight (it had an instrumental), but one was added because more information is needed. And there was almost never new information in choruses; now writers change one line per chorus — keeping the outline and first and last lines, changing the second or third line — which keeps the listener engaged. As a lyricist Murphy finds that fun ("more fun for me"). When he studies the number one records and sees something he would never have done, he has to try it — and he and his co-writers then take it a different way from the record that inspired it.

## Closing [01:47]

The more fun you have, the more money you make — but don't get into it just to make money. Murphy stresses money only because earning validates you as a dysfunctional human being. He bought his mother, the cleaning lady, a house and looked after her comfortably, and he had a great time because he was taught craft. He was not an easy sell: getting nightly reinforcement as a ten-o'clock act, he raged that his old mentors were useless and out of touch — but they cared enough to stay the course, just as their own mentors had probably endured the same from them ("this is 1922, we're the hot new kids, we have piano rolls, we have this new thing called radio"). His parting instruction: take it, use it, and somewhere down the road pass it on — help some writer who will look at you like "what do you know?" The session ends at "beer thirty."

- Fourth form's middle eight is a recent addition (replacing an instrumental) because more information is needed.
- Choruses almost never used to carry new information; now writers change one interior line per chorus to keep listeners engaged.
- Study the number one records; when they do something you'd never do, try it — then take it a different way.
- The more fun you have, the more money you make; don't get into it just for money — money's role is validation.
- Mentorship is a debt passed down generations: someone did it for your mentors, they did it for you, and you must pass it on.
