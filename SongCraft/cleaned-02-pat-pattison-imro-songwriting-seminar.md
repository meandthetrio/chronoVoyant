## No rules, only tools [00:02]

In songwriting there are no rules — **only tools** you put in your tool
belt and pull out as needed when deciding what to do next: this next
line, this next section, what options exist, and which is best.

Against the attitude that knowing things stifles creativity:
**information is the key to creativity.** What people actually mean is
that information can stifle *spontaneity* — but spontaneity and
creativity are different things. **Creativity is the ability to select,
from among many options, the option that works best with everything
else around it.** It lives in *composition* — from the Latin: to place
or arrange *with respect to the other things there*.

## Prosody: the one rule [00:05]

Aristotle (in the *Poetics*): every great work of art exhibits
**unity** — everything in the work fits together and supports everything
else to communicate the work's basic idea and emotion. Today the word is
**prosody**. If there is a rule in art, it is this: everything supports
everything else; everything in the work belongs there and has a purpose.

Working definition of prosody: **appropriate relationship between
elements.**

## The master grid: stable vs. unstable [00:07]

The most effective grid for thinking about prosody is two terms:
**stable** and **unstable**. They cover everything. An idea is either
stable ("I'm so glad to see you here") or unstable ("I'm really glad to
see you here, *but*…" — you can hear the "but" galloping over the hill
with its lance lowered; "everything's so confused now, I just don't know
what to do").

The question to ask in every section you write: **how am I feeling here —
stable or unstable?** Then use the tools so that *how the section is
built* supports *what it says*.

Every section you will ever write — verse, chorus, pre-chorus, bridge —
has the same **five properties**, always present. The only question is
whether you're aware of them and have used them to best effect:

1. Number of lines
2. Length of lines
3. Rhyme scheme
4. Rhyme types
5. Rhythm of lines (phrasing)

## Tool 1: number of lines [00:10]

Every section has either an even or an odd number of lines.

- **Odd number of lines** feels odd, incomplete, unresolved —
  *unstable*. It creates motion, and **motion creates emotion, all the
  time**: how you make something move creates its own emotion,
  independent of the words.
- **Even number of lines** feels balanced, complete — *stable*.

Example: John Mayer's "Your Body Is a Wonderland" (Grammy winner; Mayer
studied with Pattison). The chorus repeats its title line **three**
times — a three-line chorus — creating a feeling of *wanting more*,
which is exactly the situation of the song. The **final** chorus sings
the line a **fourth** time, and it feels *done*. Same words; the count
alone does the work. (Repeating one line also makes a memorable,
singable chorus.)

Match the tool to the feeling: writing an unstable idea ("you're
beautiful, I'd love to spend time with you, but I'm a broken man") in an
odd number of lines makes the *motion* feel the way the *words* feel.

**Don't premeditate this.** Premeditation makes songs sterile. It's a
rewriting tool: when a section doesn't feel right, check whether you
padded to four lines out of habit — the line that "isn't doing much
work, it must belong to a union." The four-line default comes from
Western music's love affair with symmetry (2+2=4, 4+4=8, 8+8=16 bars).

Counter-example: "Yesterday" is **seven** bars. "When you left me, you
took a whole bar out of my life." Squaring it to eight kills the song.
Things only have to balance when the *idea* is balanced.

## Tool 2: length of lines [00:20]

- **Matched line lengths** = stable. ("Eeny meeny miny mo / catch a
  tiger by the toe" — two equal lines; no itch for more.)
- **Unmatched lengths** = unstable. ("Mary had a little lamb / fleece
  was white as snow" — the shortened second line leaves the traffic cop
  waving you on: *come on, come on, finish it*.)

Paul Simon, "American Tune": "Four in the morning / crapped out,
yawning / longing my life away / I'll never worry / why should I / it's
all gonna fade." The last line is left short — where the established
rhythm has raised your expectation of one more matching phrase — and the
unresolved length delivers *existential mortality*. Balancing it off
(…"it's all gonna fade **someday**") delivers instead: life sucks, get
over it, quit whining. Same denotative meaning (the future tense already
carries "someday"); entirely different emotion. **The difference between
a stable and an unstable sequence of line lengths makes all the
difference in the emotion.**

Most writers work by default in **common meter** or tetrameter couplets
(the musical imperative of 2+2+4+4 bars again). First recognize the
rhythms you're writing in; then manipulate them. The core mechanic:
**raise the listener's expectations** with a sequence — then either
satisfy them (stability) or deliberately fail to (instability). Simon is
a master of this ("American Tune" is common meter in triplets; common
meter and its variations are everywhere).

## Tool 3: rhyme scheme [00:27]

Rhyme schemes are stable or unstable, and the scheme *by itself* creates
emotion:

- **AABB (couplets)**: pairs; the traffic cop holds up a stop sign at
  the end of line two. About as stable as it gets. ("Rock star pop star
  / sports star porn star" felt as 2+2.)
- **ABAB**: a group of four; you don't stop until line four. Line three
  functions like a **dominant chord** — expectations raised, a target
  established, pushing hard to resolution. Still stable, but it moves as
  one four-line unit. Once you've raised expectations, "they belong to
  you."
- **ABBA**: the enclosed rhyme; it says *ahh* — longing, loneliness.
  Tennyson wrote *In Memoriam*, a eulogy for his friend, in ABBA. James
  Taylor, asked his favorite of his own songs, said "Sweet Baby James" —
  *because of the rhyme scheme* (it's ABBA). The same words in ABAB
  would read as a series of facts; in ABBA they become longing.

Side observation on the eulogy tone of voice: ordinary declarative
speech sits on a tonic function; the eulogizing voice tends to sit
around the **fourth scale degree**, and ABBA matches that suspended,
unresolved quality.

There are more paradigms than these three, but all of them classify as
more or less stable or unstable.

## Tool 4: rhyme types — rhymes work like chords [00:36]

**Perfect rhyme** has three conditions:

1. The vowel sounds are identical (blood/mud — spelling is irrelevant;
   everything is sound). Accents don't break this: whatever your accent,
   you use your vowels consistently, so rhyme works the same.
2. The consonant sounds *after* the vowel (if any) are the same
   (breeze/trees — z and s spellings, same voiced z sound).
3. The syllables *begin* differently — because rhyme is a musical
   principle of tension/resolution, difference/similarity. blood/blood
   is repetition, not rhyme (an "identity").

Perfect rhyme gives **absolute closure — total stability.** But rhyme
types form a spectrum, exactly like chord voicings. His keyboard
demonstration: sing "home" over C/C (root position, most stable), then
C/G, then C/E, then E minor — each voicing still "home," but
progressively less at rest ("Mom and Dad stored their stuff in my room…
I'm sleeping in my sister's room and I don't like pink… I'm sleeping in
the basement and the house is for sale… the house has been sold, it's
five years later, and I'm peeking in the window"). **Tonic functions can
be more or less stable, and rhymes work exactly the same way:**

| Rhyme type | Example | Stability |
|---|---|---|
| Perfect | blood / mud | Total closure |
| Family (post-vowel consonants from the same phonetic family) | blood / rut | Slightly open |
| Additive / subtractive (second member adds or drops a sound) | free / bleach, bleach / free | More open |
| Assonance (vowel only) | cried / smiled | Unstable, floating |
| Consonance (vowel changes; final consonants match) | friend / wind | Least resolved; the gate wide open |

Warren Zevon, "Hasten Down the Wind": *friend / wind* is a consonance
rhyme, and it holds the ache open — "that's all right, just go, I'll be
fine, I just want you to be happy." Perfect-rhyming it (friend/bend)
would slam the gate: suddenly he just wants her out of his life. The two
lines mean the same thing; **the perfect rhyme closes the gate, the
consonance rhyme leaves it wide open** — and that difference is the
emotion.

Placement interacts with type. In an ABAB scheme (blush/skin/rush/sin):

- Weaken the **dominant position** (line 3) — blush/skin/*touch*/sin
  (family rhyme) — and the push toward line four is mitigated; you don't
  hit "sin" as hard ("you get to stop in purgatory").
- Weaken the **resolving position** (line 4) — skin/blush/sin/*touch* —
  and the push arrives but the landing is lighter; it fails to resolve
  as strongly.
- An assonance rhyme in the dominant position (skin/blush/*drift*/touch)
  barely pushes at all — the quatrain floats.
- Consonance rhymes everywhere (bluff/roar/discuss/prayer) open
  everything.

## The melody-matching problem [00:51]

A common situation: verse 1 is unstable (lost, alone), the chorus is
stable ("I met you"), and verse 2 is stable (grounded ever since). You
want to keep the melody identical between verses — so you *can't* change
the number of lines, the length of lines, or the rhyme scheme without
breaking the melodic phrases. What's left?

**In the unstable verse use unstable rhyme types; in the stable verse
use perfect rhymes.** Same line count, same lengths, same scheme, same
rhythm — different rhyme types.

Randy Newman does exactly this ("Feels Like Home"). The line "how long
I've waited for your touch" pays off with the perfect rhyme touch/much —
the door slams, "oh yeah" — where the first verse, same scheme, uses a
distant consonance rhyme (long/done, nasal family) that leaves the gate
wide open. The much-admired line isn't objectively a great line; **it
feels packed with emotion because of how it's set.** (Love/touch would
carry the same meaning but no closure.)

## Tool 5: rhythm of lines — phrasing and the downbeat [00:57]

In 4/4, the bar's four pulses have fixed relative weights: **strong —
weak — strong — weak**, the downbeat strongest of all (true of beat one
in any meter).

Two definitions:

- **Front-heavy**: a phrase that starts on (or picks up into) the
  downbeat. Its weight is at the front; it's *anchored* — the downbeat
  is a flagpole, and a flag attached to it holds in any wind. Stable.
- **Back-heavy**: a phrase that starts *after* the downbeat. Detached
  from the flagpole, in motion toward some other strong beat, weight at
  the end of the line. Unstable.

Demonstration with one repeated pair of lines — "Where are you going? /
What did I do?" — placed four different ways against the downbeat,
yielding **four different emotional landscapes**: on the downbeat it
demands an answer; after the downbeat it is puzzled, uncertain,
unstable. Depending only on the phrase's relationship to the downbeat,
the same words plead, demand, wonder, or accuse.

**Phrasing is the body language of your song.** In conversation, roughly
7% of communication is the words, 35% the tone of voice, and the rest is
body language ("I like you" with and without the eyebrows; "where's the
loo?"; "is that your daughter?"). Audience exercise ("The world goes
round and round the sun / that's just the way it is / nothing changes,
nothing's fun / without your tender kiss"): resetting which lines start
on versus after the downbeat — front-front-front-front, then
front-front-front-back, back-front-back-front, and so on — changed the
emotional color every time, with an admittedly mediocre lyric.

You are now **responsible for every downbeat in your song** — line by
line, not just section by section. Even skipping a downbeat mid-line
("nothing changes … nothing's fun" with a gap) is a different color.

**A mediocre lyric beautifully set will kick the ass, every day, of a
brilliant lyric badly set.** When Pattison first understood this he went
back to his own songs: whatever the subject — heartbreak, loss — every
phrase started on the downbeat. The songs had no body language; no
wonder they had no feeling.

## Closing [01:15]

The five tools again: number of lines, length of lines, rhyme scheme,
rhyme types, rhythm of lines. Practice them as a mad scientist — try
things and see how they feel; that's what creativity is (not
spontaneity): trying options and recognizing *that's how I feel*.

It's not about creating structure; it's about **supporting your ideas**.
The songwriter's job is to create emotion — dancing, crying, laughing —
and therefore **you must be a composer of motion in order to create
emotion.** It isn't only the chords and the melody; there are lots of
moving parts, and it's your job to find them and use them to the song's
advantage.
